# Prerequisite Concepts

Concepts and background knowledge assumed by the paper. Each entry gives a brief
explanation and a pointer to learn more.

## Major Evolutionary Transitions

**What it is:** A framework from Szathmáry and Maynard Smith (1995) arguing that the history of life consists of discrete transitions where formerly independent entities combine into new, higher-level units of organization (e.g., single cells into multicellular organisms, individuals into eusocial colonies). Each transition creates a new level at which selection and cognition operate.

**Why it matters here:** The paper's central thesis is that AI represents the next such transition — independent human and artificial minds combining into collective cognitive units, just as previous transitions yielded qualitatively new forms of intelligence.

**Learn more:** [The Major Transitions in Evolution (Smith & Szathmáry, 1997)](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-major-transitions-in-evolution-9780198502944)

## Social Brain Hypothesis

**What it is:** Robin Dunbar's hypothesis that primate brain size evolved primarily to manage complex social relationships, not to solve ecological problems. Social group size correlates with neocortex ratio across primate species.

**Why it matters here:** Supports the paper's claim that intelligence has always been fundamentally social. If brains evolved for social coordination, then scaling intelligence means scaling social complexity — not individual cognitive capacity.

**Learn more:** [Dunbar, "The Social Brain Hypothesis" (1998)](https://doi.org/10.1002/(SICI)1520-6505(1998)6:5<178::AID-EVAN5>3.0.CO;2-8)

## Cultural Ratchet

**What it is:** Michael Tomasello's concept describing how human culture accumulates knowledge across generations through imitation, teaching, and shared intentionality. Each generation inherits and builds on prior innovations without needing to reinvent them — a one-way ratchet of cultural complexity.

**Why it matters here:** The paper argues LLMs are "the cultural ratchet made computationally active" — trained on the accumulated output of human communicative exchange, they externalize social cognition onto a new substrate.

**Learn more:** [Tomasello, The Cultural Origins of Human Cognition (1999)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674005822)

## Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)

**What it is:** A technique for aligning language models with human preferences by training a reward model on human comparisons of model outputs, then optimizing the language model against that reward signal.

**Why it matters here:** The paper critiques RLHF as a fundamentally dyadic (parent-child) correction mechanism that cannot scale to ecosystems of billions of agents. It serves as the foil for the proposed alternative: institutional alignment.

**Learn more:** [Christiano et al., "Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences" (NeurIPS 2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03741)

## Institutional Alignment

**What it is:** The paper's proposed alternative to RLHF-style alignment. Inspired by Elinor Ostrom's work on governing the commons and Douglass North's institutional economics, it envisions alignment achieved through persistent institutional structures — defined roles, norms, and protocols — rather than individual-level value training. Agents conform to role protocols (like "judge" or "auditor") regardless of their individual properties.

**Why it matters here:** This is the paper's core policy proposal for scaling AI governance. It shifts alignment from a training-time problem to a deployment-time organizational design problem.

**Learn more:** [Ostrom, Governing the Commons (1990)](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511807763)

## Collective Intelligence

**What it is:** The capacity of groups to perform cognitive tasks better than individuals. Woolley et al. (2010) demonstrated a measurable "collective intelligence factor" in human groups that predicts group performance across diverse tasks, analogous to individual IQ but at the group level.

**Why it matters here:** Provides empirical grounding for the claim that intelligence is a group-level property. The paper argues this research should inform multi-agent AI system design but has been largely ignored by the AI community.

**Learn more:** [Woolley et al., "Evidence for a Collective Intelligence Factor" (Science, 2010)](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1193147)

## Argumentative Theory of Reasoning

**What it is:** Mercier and Sperber's theory that human reasoning evolved not for individual problem-solving but for argumentation — producing and evaluating arguments in social contexts. Reasoning biases (like confirmation bias) are features, not bugs, of a system designed for persuasion and evaluation in groups.

**Why it matters here:** Directly supports the "society of thought" finding. If reasoning is fundamentally argumentative/social, then models spontaneously developing internal debates under optimization pressure is a convergent rediscovery of this principle.

**Learn more:** [Mercier & Sperber, The Enigma of Reason (2017)](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674237827)
